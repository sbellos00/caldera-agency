Part 1 of 3

Homepage Breakdown.

Hero · On Load

Content needs to fit inside the viewport

When the page loads, the banner looks good, but on most screens the only thing in the viewport is “Chad Pollitt.” The line that positions you (“Global Keynote Speaker, Fractional CMO, Professor & Author”) and both call-to-action buttons sit below the fold, so the visitor has to scroll to see them. It may seem trivial, but it’s important: the user has to “get it” immediately, without taking any action to understand what they’re seeing.

Recommended improvement:

Rebuild the hero so the positioning line and one clear button are visible the instant the page loads, with no scrolling required. Who you help, what you do, and how to book you, all above the fold.

Hero · Color & Contrast

Hero content color contrast

As you scroll, the text and buttons start to get lost in the photo behind them, especially where they pass over the bright labels in the image. The hover animation on the buttons also creates a red-on-red effect (a problem in other sections too).

This is important information and needs to stay clearly readable. It’s the site’s first impression, and at this stage the user has low commitment. If reading the page takes effort, they’re more likely to bounce, so we need to make the experience smooth here, and keep things easy to act on.

Recommended improvement:

Fix the contrast properly: a treatment or shadow behind the text so it always reads, and buttons in a color that stands off the background and clearly says “click me.” Legibility first, everywhere.

Mid Page · Image Sections

The photos are faded, and white text on top is hard to read

Scrolling down, several of the photo sections have a washed-out, light-grey tint over them. Combined with white text laid on top, it gets hard to read.

Recommended improvement:

Stronger, cleaner imagery without the grey wash, and text contrast that holds up no matter what’s behind it.

Mid Page · The Two Large Boxes

Two of the most important links don’t look like links

The two large blocks (“Have Chad at your next event” and “Download Chad’s books & resources”) do lead somewhere, but nothing about them signals that they’re clickable. A visitor reads them as headings, not buttons, and scrolls past two of your key conversion points.

Recommended improvement:

Make them obviously interactive: clear button styling, hover behavior, and a label that tells you what happens when you click.

Mid Page · “Inquire Now”

The main booking button doesn’t grab attention

The “Book a keynote, consult, or fractional CMO service” section ends in an “Inquire Now” button whose color doesn’t stand out from everything around it. This is the one thing that should immediately grab attention, and right now the contrast makes it easy to miss.

Recommended improvement:

Give the primary action a color and weight that pulls the eye straight to it. This is the most important button on the page.

Mid Page · Structural Change

Too Many Calls to Action in the Same Viewport

In this section, there are three calls to action appearing very close together: the two clickable photo blocks and the “Inquire Now” button directly underneath them.

This creates a structure issue because two of the actions lead to the contact form, while the other leads to Books & Resources. As a result, the visitor is being shown multiple competing paths at the same time, which can weaken the clarity of the section.

Recommended improvement:

We should simplify this section by keeping two clear actions: one for Books & Resources and one for the contact form.

This would make the section easier to understand and give the visitor a clearer choice without overcrowding the page with competing calls to action.

Mid Page · Speaking Video

The speaking video has a great effect and no sound

The clip of you speaking, framed with the TV effect, looks very good, but it isn’t functional, because there’s no audio. Watching someone speak to a room with no sound is confusing, and it wastes a chance to let visitors see you in action.

Recommended improvement:

Give it sound, and place it so it invites visitors to press play.

Mid Page · Accomplishments

Proof points are too subtle

The accomplishments block (24 years, 12 books, 100+ talks, 100k+ brands) is a very strong selling point that we should lean into. I’d strongly suggest moving it higher up the page; it’s some of the clearest evidence you have, and you want as many visitors as possible to see it. Rather than leaving it as static text, we can animate it so it draws the eye.

Recommended improvement:

Move it higher and animate it: counters that tick up as you scroll, a bit of motion, so the numbers get noticed instead of skimmed past.

Mid Page · Photo “Posts”

The clickable photo cards are confusing

The images that open into a short post-style caption are a clever way to add context. But the photos are faded and too crowded, and the grey font is hard to read.

Recommended improvement:

Keep the concept but polish it: sharp images, readable type, and a layout that makes each one feel worth clicking.

Mid Page · Testimonial

One static testimonial where there should be a wall of proof

Right now there’s a single testimonial: static text and a photo. For someone with your track record, that’s a fraction of the credibility you could be showing.

Recommended improvement:

Build a testimonial carousel that pairs each quote with a photo. You have very strong social proof, so lean into it.

Mid Page · Speaking Engagements

The stock photo looks a bit off

The “Speaking Engagements” section sits on a black, grey, and white stock image of a microphone, with white text over it. Contrast is a problem here too, and it makes the text hard to read. It also doesn’t fit the rest of the page design.

Recommended improvement:

Replace it with a real shot of you on stage (you have plenty) and fix the text contrast so it reads instantly.

Part 2 of 3

Sitewide Priorities

From here on, we highlight the broader patterns that repeat across the site. We avoided going line by line for the rest of the site like we did on the homepage, we didn’t want to send you a 40 page document that takes 1 hour to review.

1.

Improve Text Legibility Across Key Pages

In several sections, the background imagery and text colors compete with each other, which makes important content harder to read.

This appears most noticeably in:

  1. Services page: Hero section
  2. About page: Hero section
  3. Other image-based sections where text sits directly on top of photography or visually busy backgrounds
Recommended improvement:

We should adjust these sections so the text stays clearly readable against the background. This can be done by improving contrast, adding a cleaner overlay where needed, adjusting the text color, or repositioning the copy so it does not sit on top of visually busy areas.

These are important first-impression sections, so the visitor should be able to understand the message immediately without needing to work around the background image or color treatment.

2.

Make Navigation and Buttons Clearer

Some buttons and clickable elements are not immediately recognizable as navigation items or calls to action. This can make it less obvious where the visitor should click next.

Recommended improvement:

We should make buttons, links, and navigation elements more visually distinct, consistent, and easy to identify.

The goal is for visitors to immediately understand what is clickable, what each action means, and where each click will take them.

3.

Strengthen Visual and Brand Consistency

There are a few areas where the visual style feels inconsistent across the website. Some sections use one-off heading styles, hover treatments, animation styles, or typography choices that do not feel fully connected to the rest of the site.

Recommended improvement:

We should simplify and standardize the visual system across the website.

This includes the typography, heading styles, button treatments, animations, spacing, and section layouts. If multiple styles are being used intentionally, they should follow a clear hierarchy and repeat consistently. Otherwise, the site can start to feel less unified.

The goal is to make the full website feel more polished, cohesive, and intentional.

4.

Improve Layout and Reading Flow

Some sections contain valuable content, but the layout does not always guide the eye clearly enough. As a result, important information can feel visually equal to less important details, which makes the page harder to scan.

This is most noticeable in:

  1. The four listed services
  2. The About page overall
  3. The “Other Notables” section/list
  4. The Book section, where the left side feels text-heavy while the right side has more breathing room and feels easier to follow
  5. Several text-heavy sections where the hierarchy between primary and secondary information is not clear enough
Recommended improvement:

We should create a stronger visual hierarchy across these sections.

The most important information should stand out first, while supporting details should feel secondary. This can be improved through spacing, section structure, typography, layout balance, and clearer content grouping.

The goal is to make the site easier to scan, easier to follow, and more seamless to read.

5.

Clarify the Book & Resources Section

The Book & Resources page currently feels text-heavy, especially on the left side. It also takes too long to understand the purpose of signing up.

At the moment, the visitor needs to read through several paragraphs before it becomes clear that signing up may relate to the newsletter, blog content, and resources.

Recommended improvement:

We should make the value of signing up much clearer and faster to understand.

The section should immediately explain what the visitor gets and why it matters. A clearer structure could separate:

  1. Newsletter
  2. Blog updates
  3. Downloadable or available resources

This would make the page easier to understand at a glance and reduce friction for visitors.

6.

Fix Technical and Performance Issues

There are a few technical and performance issues that should be reviewed and corrected.

Current issues noticed:

  1. Video and media behavior should be reviewed across the site, including sound, loading, and performance
  2. The navigation bar disappears in the Book section
  3. Some videos appear to lag
  4. Mobile responsiveness needs significant improvement
Recommended improvement:

We should review the full technical experience across desktop and mobile.

This includes video behavior, page performance, navigation behavior, loading smoothness, and responsiveness across different screen sizes. Mobile is especially important because many visitors will experience the site first from their phones.

The goal is to make the website feel smooth, stable, and easy to use across every major device.

7.

Improve Contact and Booking Flow

At the moment, clicking “Contact” simply scrolls down to the contact form rather than opening a dedicated contact page or booking experience. This works as a basic contact path, but it could be much clearer for visitors who are ready to reach out with a specific type of inquiry.

A booking system with clear request paths would make it easier for the right inquiry to reach you. For example, visitors could choose between speaking inquiries, fractional work, consulting requests, or other general inquiries.

Recommended improvement:

We should add a clearer booking system so ready buyers and serious inquiries have a more direct path.

This would make the website easier to use and help visitors understand exactly what type of request they should make.

The Goal

The goal of these changes is to make the website feel clearer, more polished, and easier for visitors to move through, while also making your accomplishments and credibility more visible. In some places this means redesigning sections from the ground up rather than simply tweaking what is already there.

The current site already contains strong content, experience, and proof of credibility. The opportunity is to present those elements with stronger structure, clearer hierarchy, and a more intentional design system.
